Words that are easy to confuse or use incorrectly
make vs do
very common mistake
We "make" things that are created. We "do" things that are tasks or actions.
tap for examples ↓
✗ "I did a mistake."
✓ "I made a mistake."
✗ "She made her homework."
✓ "She did her homework."
say vs tell
very common mistake
"Say" does not need a person after it. "Tell" always needs a person.
tap for examples ↓
✗ "She said me to stop."
✓ "She told me to stop."
✗ "He told that he was tired."
✓ "He said that he was tired."
in / on / at (place)
preposition
at = exact spot · on = surface or transport · in = inside a space
tap for examples ↓
✓ "I'm at work / at the office."
✓ "She's on the bus / on the train."
✓ "We work in Ankara / in the lab."
in / on / at (time)
preposition
at = exact time · on = day or date · in = month, year, season
tap for examples ↓
✓ "at 8am · at night · at the weekend"
✓ "on Monday · on 18 April · on my birthday"
✓ "in April · in 2024 · in the morning"
interested vs interesting
easy to swap!
"Interested" = how YOU feel · "Interesting" = what the thing is like
tap for examples ↓
✗ "The subject is very interested."
✓ "The subject is very interesting."
✓ "I am very interested in chemistry."
bored vs boring
easy to swap!
"Bored" = how YOU feel · "Boring" = what the thing is like
tap for examples ↓
✗ "The meeting was very bored."
✓ "The meeting was very boring."
✓ "I was so bored in the meeting."
since vs for
with perfect tenses
"Since" = a point in time · "For" = a length of time
tap for examples ↓
✗ "I've worked here since two years."
✓ "I've worked here for two years."
✓ "I've worked here since 2022."
I / me / my
pronouns
"I" = subject (doing the action) · "me" = object (receiving) · "my" = possession
tap for examples ↓
✗ "Me and my colleague presented."
✓ "My colleague and I presented."
✗ "She sent the report to I."
✓ "She sent the report to me."
used to
past habit
Use "used to" for things that were true in the past but are not now.
tap for examples ↓
✗ "I use to work in a different company."
✓ "I used to work in a different company."
✓ "She used to live in Istanbul."
remember to vs remember -ing
verb patterns
"Remember to do" = don't forget · "Remember doing" = recall a past moment
tap for examples ↓
✓ "Remember to send the report." (future task — don't forget!)
✓ "I remember sending the report." (I did it; I recall it)
to feel vs to think
expressing opinion
Both can express opinions, but "feel" is more personal/emotional. "Think" is more neutral.
tap for examples ↓
✓ "I think the exam will be fine." (neutral opinion)
✓ "I feel nervous about speaking." (emotional)
✗ "I feel that the answer is B." (awkward — use "think" here)
quite vs very
degree adverbs
"Quite" in British English often means "fairly/moderately", not "very". Be careful!
tap for examples ↓
✓ "It was quite good." (= fairly good, not amazing)
✓ "It was very good." (= strongly good)
In Turkish, "oldukça" = quite. But in English, "quite" is softer than you might expect.
