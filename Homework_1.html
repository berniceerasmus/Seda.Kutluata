Why this matters for your exam

The İTEP grammar section will ask you to choose the correct tense for a sentence. Present perfect and past perfect are two of the most commonly tested tenses — and they are easy to confuse! The good news is that once you understand the logic behind them, they start to feel natural.

Present perfect — the basics

Use present perfect when: something happened in the past but is still connected to the present — you can feel its result right now.

Think of it as a bridge between the past and the present.

When does it happen? — visualise this
Experience (ever / never)
Something that happened at an unspecified time in the past
"I have visited London." / "She has never eaten sushi."
Recent past with present result
Something just happened and you can see/feel the result now
"I have lost my keys." (= I can't find them now) / "She has had a baby." (= the baby is here now)
Duration up to now (for / since)
Something started in the past and continues now
"I have worked here for two years." / "She has lived in Ankara since 2018."
📐
Formula — present perfect
Subject + have / has + past participle
"I have worked…"  ·  "She has started…"  ·  "They have finished…"
Key words → present perfect
already · just · yet · ever · never · for · since · recently · so far
"Have you eaten yet?" / "I've just arrived."
have vs has — don't mix them!
I / you / we / they → have
he / she / it → has
"She has started." / "We have started."
Past perfect — the basics

Use past perfect when: you are talking about the past, and you need to describe something that happened even earlier — before another past event.

Think of it as the "past of the past." If you have two past events and one happened before the other, the earlier one uses past perfect.

Visualise the order of events
Event 1 — earlier past (past perfect)
This happened first. Use had + past participle.
"She had already left…"
Event 2 — later past (simple past)
This happened second. Use normal past tense.
"…when I arrived."
📐
Formula — past perfect
Subject + had + past participle
"I had finished…"  ·  "She had already left…"  ·  "They had never met…"
Key words → past perfect
already · just · never · before · after · by the time · when
"By the time I arrived, she had left." / "I had never tried it before."
The big difference
Present perfect → connected to now
Past perfect → connected to another past moment
"I have eaten." (I'm full now) vs "I had eaten." (before something else happened)
Common mistakes — read these carefully!
"I have seen her yesterday."
"I saw her yesterday." — when you say a specific time (yesterday), use simple past, not present perfect!
"She already left when I called."
"She had already left when I called." — two past events: left happened first, so use past perfect.
"I worked here since 2022."
"I have worked here since 2022." — still working now, so use present perfect.

Why these three are tricky

In Turkish, one word often covers what English splits into three. This means your brain is searching for one rule, but English has three! The good news is that the rules are logical — once you see the pattern, it clicks.

Think of them as circles of size: AT is the smallest (a specific point) · ON is medium (a surface or line) · IN is the largest (inside a space)

The rules — place
AT ON IN
A specific point or location
at the office, at home, at the lab, at the door, at work		 A surface, or transport
on the table, on the bus, on the train, on the computer, on the phone		 Inside a space, city, country
in Ankara, in Turkey, in the room, in the lab, in the car
The rules — time
AT ON IN
Specific time or holiday
at 8am, at noon, at night, at the weekend, at Easter		 Day or specific date
on Monday, on 18 April, on my birthday, on weekdays		 Month, year, season, longer period
in April, in 2024, in the morning, in summer, in the future
AT — think: exact point
Smallest reference. You can point to it on a map or clock.
"I start at 8am." / "She's at the office."
ON — think: surface / line
Days are "lines" in your calendar. Surfaces are flat.
"On Monday morning." / "It's on the table."
IN — think: container / space
You are inside something — a city, a room, a period of time.
"I live in Ankara." / "In the morning."
Common mistakes
"I start work in 8am."
"I start work at 8am." — specific clock time = AT
"I'll see you in Monday."
"I'll see you on Monday." — day of the week = ON
"She works on the R&D department."
"She works in the R&D department." — inside a department/place = IN
"I'll call you on the morning."
"I'll call you in the morning." — longer period of day = IN

Why this works
The goal is not to be perfect — it's to get your brain used to producing English directly. Right now, your brain translates from Turkish first. Speaking out loud every day, even for 5 minutes, trains a shortcut that bypasses the translation step. Over time it becomes automatic. The car on your commute is perfect for this — no one is listening, no pressure!
How to do it — simple steps

Step 1: Choose the day's topic below (or make up your own).

Step 2: Use the sentence starter if you get stuck — then keep going.

Step 3: Speak for at least 3–5 sentences without stopping. If you don't know a word, describe it instead!

Step 4: Don't correct yourself as you go. Finish the idea first, then you can go back.

Useful phrases when you get stuck

These are natural phrases that real English speakers use when they need a moment. Use them in the exam too — they sound confident, not hesitant.

Buying yourself time
"That's a good question…" · "Let me think about that for a second…" · "Well, to be honest…"
When you forget a word
"The thing you use for…" · "It's similar to…" · "I can't remember the exact word, but it means…"
Adding to your answer
"What's more…" · "Also…" · "Another thing I'd like to add is…" · "For example, in my case…"
Finishing strongly
"So overall, I think…" · "To sum up…" · "That's why I believe…"
